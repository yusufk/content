# The Automation Ladder: A New Career Framework for Engineering

## The Problem with Traditional Promotions

Most engineering career ladders are broken. They reward people for *doing* more complex work, not for *eliminating* work. The result? Senior engineers become bottlenecks. Knowledge hoarders get promoted. The team doesn't scale.

What if we flipped it?

## The Proposal: Automate Yourself Out of Your Role

The rule is simple: **you don't get promoted until you can automate what you do in your current role.**

A junior engineer writes code. To become a mid-level engineer, they must build systems — templates, generators, CI pipelines, abstractions — that allow the *next* junior engineer to do what they did without needing them.

A mid-level engineer designs systems. To become senior, they must encode their design decisions into architecture decision records, platform tooling, and guardrails that let mid-level engineers make those same decisions autonomously.

A senior engineer sets technical direction. To become a staff engineer, they must build the frameworks — technical strategy docs, evaluation rubrics, RFC processes — that let seniors set direction without them in the room.

## The Levels

| Level | Core Work | Promotion Criteria |
|-------|-----------|-------------------|
| Junior | Write code to spec | Automate repetitive coding patterns; create reusable components/templates |
| Mid | Design solutions | Encode design decisions into tooling, linters, platform abstractions |
| Senior | Set technical direction | Build frameworks that let others set direction (RFC processes, tech radar, decision rubrics) |
| Staff | Shape org-wide strategy | Build systems of influence that outlast your involvement |

## Why This Works

**1. It rewards force multiplication, not individual heroics.**
The person who automates themselves out of a job has proven they understand the job deeply enough to systematise it. That's exactly who you want at the next level.

**2. It creates a natural scaling mechanism.**
Each promotion literally increases the team's capacity. The ladder itself becomes a growth engine.

**3. It kills knowledge hoarding.**
You can't get promoted by being the only person who knows how something works. You get promoted by making that knowledge accessible to everyone.

**4. It aligns incentives with engineering excellence.**
Good engineering *is* automation. We automate for users — why not automate for ourselves?

## Why Now?

This idea isn't new — good engineers have always automated themselves out of jobs. But until recently, the *ceiling* on what you could automate was low. You could write scripts, build CI pipelines, create templates. Useful, but limited.

AI changes everything.

With LLMs, code generation, and agentic tooling, an engineer can now genuinely automate *cognitive* work — not just repetitive tasks. A mid-level engineer can build an AI-assisted design review tool that encodes senior-level judgment. A senior can create an architecture advisor that guides teams through decisions they'd previously need a staff engineer for.

The automation ladder was always the right framework. AI just removed the excuse that it wasn't practical. The tools now exist to automate not just the *mechanical* parts of your role, but the *thinking* parts too.

This means the bar for promotion becomes both more achievable and more meaningful. You're not just writing a script — you're encoding expertise into systems that genuinely multiply your team's capability.

## The Hard Questions

**What about roles that can't be fully automated?**
They don't need to be *fully* automated. The bar is: can someone at your current level do 80% of what you do without your direct involvement? The remaining 20% is what makes the next level challenging.

**Doesn't this just describe good engineering?**
Yes. That's the point. Most career ladders reward things that aren't good engineering — politics, visibility, tenure. This one rewards the thing we actually value.

**What about the human side — mentoring, culture, collaboration?**
Automation doesn't mean removing humans. A mentoring programme is automation — it's a system that develops people without requiring a single senior engineer to carry all the load. Documentation is automation. Onboarding processes are automation.

## The Laziness Trap

There's an obvious failure mode: someone uses AI to generate the automation artifacts without ever understanding the work they're automating. They produce templates, tooling, and abstractions that *look* like force multiplication but are actually cargo-culted output.

The defence is built into the framework itself:

**1. You can't automate what you don't understand.**
If your "automation" breaks and you can't debug it, you haven't automated anything — you've created a liability. The promotion bar should include *maintaining and evolving* what you built, not just shipping it.

**2. The output has to actually work for others.**
If the next junior can't use your templates effectively, you haven't met the bar. Automation that only works when you're babysitting it isn't automation.

**3. Peer review of the automation itself.**
The same way we review code, we should review the systems people build to replace themselves. Does this encode real judgment or just surface patterns?

**4. The "explain it without the tool" test.**
Can you explain the design decision your linter enforces? Can you articulate the architecture pattern your template implements? If not, you didn't automate expertise — you outsourced thinking.

The automation ladder doesn't reward *using* AI. It rewards *teaching* AI (and systems) to do what you genuinely know how to do. There's a world of difference between "I asked Copilot to generate a CI pipeline" and "I designed a CI pipeline that encodes our team's deployment philosophy, and I used AI to help implement it faster."

## The Mindset Shift

Traditional ladder: "I'm too valuable to replace."
Automation ladder: "I've made myself replaceable — that's why I'm ready for more."

The best engineers I've worked with already think this way. They build themselves out of jobs. They leave behind systems, not dependencies. This framework just makes that the explicit expectation.
